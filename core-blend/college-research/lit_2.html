2.1 Introduction

This literature review comprehensively examines existing scholarly works and research studies on the utilization of college curriculum in practical environments. It emphasizes the importance of understanding how college curriculum is applied in real-world settings and its impact on students' preparedness for the professional field. The review identifies key themes, theories, and findings, addresses gaps in current knowledge, and contributes to the existing literature. By synthesizing relevant studies, it establishes a foundation for the subsequent chapters and informs the research study on examining the utilization of college curriculum in practical environments.

2.2 Review of Concepts and Variables

This section critically examines and analyzes the key concepts and variables relevant to examining the utilization of college curriculum in practical environments. It provides a comprehensive understanding of the conceptual framework and variables used in previous research studies. The study explores and analyzes several important concepts and variables.

One of these concepts is the College Curriculum, which refers to the various courses, assignments, and learning experiences provided by educational institutions to students pursuing higher education.

Another concept is Practical Environments, encompassing real-world settings like workplaces, industries, or professional fields where individuals apply the knowledge and skills they acquire through their college curriculum.

The concept of Utilization examines how effectively and efficiently individuals can apply the knowledge and skills gained from their college curriculum in practical environments, considering the extent to which the acquired knowledge is put into practice.

Additionally, the study considers the variable of Employability, which assesses the extent to which individuals' alignment with industry needs, influenced by their college curriculum, contributes to their potential for finding employment and succeeding in their chosen careers.

The variable Relevance is also examined, evaluating the degree to which the content and skills taught in the college curriculum align with the requirements and demands of practical environments, thereby preparing individuals for real-world challenges.

Lastly, the variable of Effectiveness measures how successfully individuals' application of their college curriculum in practical environments leads to positive outcomes, including improved job performance, problem-solving abilities, and adaptability.

2.3 Theoretical Framework

The Theoretical Framework presents a collection of relevant theories from various disciplines. These theories provide a conceptual foundation for understanding the relationship between college curriculum and its application in real-world settings. By drawing upon established psychological, organizational behavior, and educational theories together with conceptual models, this section aims to explore how individuals acquire, transfer, and utilize knowledge and skills from their college education in practical contexts. The inclusion of these theories in the theoretical framework helps to guide the analysis and interpretation of the research findings, ultimately contributing to a deeper understanding of how college curriculum can effectively prepare individuals for the demands and challenges of the professional field.

These are theories from the field of education associated with practical relevance of university coursework:

  • Constructivism: This theory suggests that individuals actively construct knowledge through their experiences and interactions with the environment. It emphasizes the importance of hands-on learning and practical application of knowledge.

    In the context of utilizing college curriculum in practical environments, Constructivism emphasizes the importance of active engagement, problem-solving, and connecting theory to real-world situations. By incorporating teaching methods that promote application, collaboration, and reflection, constructivism enhances students' ability to utilize their learning effectively in practical settings. It emphasizes authentic, meaningful, and situated learning experiences that prepare students for the demands of the professional field.

  • Experiential Learning Theory: Proposed by David Kolb, this theory highlights the role of experience in the learning process. It suggests that individuals learn best through direct experiences and active engagement, reflect on those experiences, draw conclusions, and actively apply their knowledge in practical contexts.

    In the context of utilizing college curriculum in practical environments, Experiential Learning emphasizes the value of hands-on, real-world experiences in applying theoretical knowledge. It promotes the idea that learning is enhanced when students actively participate in problem-solving, reflection, and application of concepts in practical settings. By engaging in experiential learning opportunities, such as practical internships, fieldwork, or simulations, students develop practical skills, critical thinking abilities, and a deeper understanding of how their college curriculum can be utilized effectively in real-world contexts.

  • Transfer of Learning: This theory explores how individuals apply knowledge and skills learned in one context to new situations. It examines factors that facilitate or hinder the transfer of learning from the college curriculum to practical environments.

    In the context of utilizing college curriculum in practical environments, Transfer of Learning highlights students' capacity to apply their academic learning in real-world scenarios. It emphasizes the transferability of knowledge and skills from college courses to practical environments. This process involves students adapting their learning to solve problems, make decisions, and perform tasks. By promoting the transfer of learning, educators can enhance the practical relevance of college curriculum, better equipping students for professional challenges and demands.

    In the same context of Transfer of Learning, several factors can hinder the successful transfer of learning:

    • Contextual Differences: Differences in the context, setting, or circumstances between the college learning environment and the practical field can make it challenging to apply acquired knowledge and skills effectively.
    • Lack of Relevance: If students perceive a lack of relevance or connection between what they learned in college and the demands of the practical environment, they may struggle to transfer their learning appropriately.
    • Limited Practice Opportunities: Insufficient opportunities for students to practice and apply their learning in real-world scenarios can hinder the transfer of knowledge and skills to practical settings.
    • Inadequate Guidance and Support: Insufficient guidance or support from educators and mentors in bridging the gap between college learning and practical application can impede the transfer of learning.
    • Fixed Mindset: A fixed mindset, where individuals believe their abilities are fixed and cannot be developed, can limit the transfer of learning as it discourages exploration and application of knowledge in new contexts.
    • Time Constraints: Limited time available for students to reflect, integrate, and consolidate their learning from college courses into practical situations can hinder the transfer of learning.
    • Lack of Reflection and Metacognition: Inadequate opportunities for students to reflect on their learning, engage in metacognitive processes, and consciously apply their knowledge and skills in practical environments can hinder the transfer of learning. Metacognition is the ability to think about and be aware of your own thoughts, knowledge, and learning strategies.

  • Authentic Learning: This theory promotes learning experiences that closely resemble real-world situations. It emphasizes the importance of meaningful and authentic tasks that mirror the challenges and complexities of the practical environments to prepare individuals for the real-world.

    In the context of utilizing college curriculum in practical environment, it promotes the integration of authentic tasks, projects, and assessments that require students to apply their knowledge and skills in realistic contexts. By engaging in authentic learning experiences, students develop a deeper understanding of the subject matter, enhance their problem-solving and critical thinking abilities, and improve their preparedness for the demands of the professional field.

  • Situated Learning: This theory emphasizes the social and contextual aspects of learning. It suggests that learning occurs through participation in authentic activities within a community of practice, where individuals acquire and apply knowledge in meaningful ways.

    In the context of utilizing college curriculum in practical environment, by immersing students in situated learning experiences, such as internships, fieldwork, or simulations, they can develop a deeper understanding of how to apply their knowledge and skills in practical settings. This theory emphasizes the value of learning through active participation and collaboration, allowing students to acquire and utilize knowledge within the specific contexts in which it is relevant.

  • Social Cognitive Theory: This theory, proposed by Albert Bandura, emphasizes the influence of social interactions and observational learning on individual learning and behavior. It explores how individuals acquire and apply knowledge and skills by observing and modeling others in practical settings.

    The Social Cognitive theory emphasizes observation, modeling, and social interaction in the learning process. Students learn by observing and imitating successful role models in practical settings. Social learning experiences, like group projects and mentoring, enhance the utilization of college curriculum by promoting interaction and learning from others. This theory recognizes the influence of social factors on learning and encourages integrating social dynamics into the utilization of college curriculum in practical environments.

2.4 Empirical Review of Literature

The empirical review of literature in this chapter provides an analysis of relevant empirical studies and research findings pertaining to the utilization of college curriculum in practical environments. This section aims to examine the existing body of empirical research conducted on the topic, with a focus on identifying key trends, themes, and insights related to the utilization of college curriculum in real-world settings. By systematically reviewing and analyzing the empirical studies, this review aims to contribute to a deeper understanding of how college curriculum is utilized in practical environments and its impact on students' preparedness for the professional field.

The topic of utilizing college coursework in practical environments is a subject that has received relatively little attention in terms of research studies and articles. The scarcity of relevant literature directly addressing this topic has resulted in only a select few sources being highlighted. Consequently, there exists a significant gap in our knowledge base regarding the effectiveness and relevance of college coursework in practical settings. It is essential for future research endeavors to delve into this underexplored area, shedding light on the intricacies and implications of integrating academic learning with practical experiences, thus providing valuable insights for educators, students, and employers.

Research by the University of Chicago conducted by Carly Kontra, Daniel J. Lyons, Susan M. Fischer, and Sian L. Beilock on physical experience enhances science learning unveiled some insights regarding experiential learning. According to the study, physical experience with the material world enhances learning and aids understanding of complex science concepts involving kinetics. The study specifically focused on the concept of angular momentum in physics education. The researchers conducted laboratory experiments and a field experiment in a college physics class to investigate the importance of physical experience in science learning.

The results showed that students who physically experienced forces associated with angular momentum performed significantly better on quizzes compared to those who only observed the consequences of angular momentum. Furthermore, brain imaging data revealed that physical experience led to the activation of sensorimotor brain systems involved in computing forces, vectors, and trajectories. This activation of sensorimotor brain regions contributed to students' improved understanding of angular momentum. This research finding emphasizes the value of incorporating physical experience and hands-on activities in science education. It suggests that providing students with opportunities to engage with the physical world and manipulate objects related to the concepts they are learning can enhance their understanding and learning outcomes.

This finding highlights the importance of incorporating physical experiences and real-world applications of the curriculum to enhance students' understanding and application of scientific concepts.

The research by M. L. Ronksley-Pavia on curriculum differentiation, revealed some findings in relation to utilizing coursework in the practical field. The research finding emphasizes the concept of curriculum differentiation for gifted and talented students. It defines curriculum differentiation as the modification of the regular curriculum by adjusting processes, skills, content, and learning environments to suit the needs of gifted and talented students. The study suggests that curriculum differentiation should include extension and enrichment programs to broaden the curriculum and develop students' skills and abilities to a level of complexity aligned with their cognitive abilities. Various methods for differentiating the curriculum are highlighted, including assessing learners' prior skills, utilizing tiered tasks, acceleration for independent study, flexible grouping, and planning autonomous research tasks.

The research finding also introduces curriculum differentiation models, such as Kaplan's and Williams' models, which demonstrate how content, instruction, and learning processes can be adjusted to meet the educational needs of gifted learners. Furthermore, the research finding highlights the importance of individualizing the differentiated curriculum to reflect the needs, learning abilities, and interests of individual gifted students.

Research by Kandiko Howson and Kingsbury on curriculum change revealed departments' transformation from a content-based, high-stakes exam approach to incorporating broader institutional aims and active learning theories. The study used transformational learning theory to analyze department engagement and adoption of new strategies. Three stages of departmental engagement were identified using an evaluation rubric, providing a framework to assess curriculum change initiatives. The research emphasizes the importance of integrating institutional strategy and disciplinary expertise, highlighting language adoption as crucial for successful implementation. The study underscores the complexity of implementing curriculum change and the need to align practices with institutional goals and active learning theories.

The research finding by Tamil Selvan reveals how the implementation of curriculum plays a crucial role in achieving the goals and success of the curriculum. A well-prepared teacher is essential for effective curriculum implementation. This finding suggests that teacher preparedness and competence are key factors in ensuring successful curriculum implementation. It highlights the importance of providing teachers with appropriate training and support to effectively execute curriculum plans and deliver quality education to students so they can be able to apply it in the real world.

A teacher who has prepared well enough with the comprehensive understanding of both theoretical knowledge and practical skills prior to teaching can effectively equip students with the necessary capabilities to apply them in real-world scenarios.

The research by Tovar-Gálvez, J. C. on bringing environmental education to the Curriculum also made some key findings in relation to application of university coursework in professional contexts. The study highlighted selecting local environmental situations to contextualize the curriculum. The cases demonstrate the importance of integrating local environmental problems into the curriculum to make the content more meaningful and encourage students to engage with their surroundings. Guiding teaching and learning through questions play a crucial role in integrating environmental problems into the curriculum and motivating students' learning. They facilitate dialogue, present challenges, and encourage students to better understand and transform their reality.

The experiences highlight the limitations imposed by traditional curricular conditions and institutional barriers. Institutions should support teachers in integrating environmental education into the curriculum by understanding and managing its complexity and fostering curricular flexibility.

The research by Yangtao Kong on the role of experiential learning on students’ motivation and classroom engagement made some key findings in relation to the experiential learning theory. Experiential learning (EL) plays a significant role in enhancing students' classroom engagement and motivation. The use of EL methods in education promotes active learning, real-world application of knowledge, and autonomy in problem-solving. Students who actively participate in experiential activities show higher levels of engagement and intrinsic motivation, leading to improved learning outcomes. EL bridges the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical skills, providing students with opportunities to apply their learning in relevant contexts. Implementing EL in-teaching practices can contribute to creating a positive and engaging learning environment.

Finally, the study by Graham D Bodie and Susanne M. Jones revealed that constructivism has been used to understand the development of social cognitive and communication skills in children and adolescents. It implies that students can actively engage with the college curriculum to apply it in practical environments by actively reconstructing their environment. This perspective implies that students should be encouraged to actively engage with the curriculum, seek practical applications, and actively participate in practical environments to make the most of their college education. Cognitive complexity, linked to constructivism, enhances social perception and information processing. Students with higher cognitive complexity can effectively understand and utilize the college curriculum in practical environments, as they adeptly evaluate social interactions and adapt messages accordingly. In the study, motivation shows to play a role in the production of person-centered messages. Students who are motivated to achieve specific outcomes and have confidence in their abilities may be more likely to actively utilize the college curriculum in practical environments and apply their knowledge effectively.
